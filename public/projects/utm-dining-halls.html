Introduction

Overview

The University of Toronto, Mississauga has three dining options that serve nearly 15,000 students every day. On-campus dining halls play an important part in the health of UTM's students. For many, on-campus dining is a necessity – they live on campus and do not have easy access to other options. For others, on-campus dining is a convenient way to eat before, after, or in-between classes. Despite its importance, using one of UTM's three dining options is a time-consuming endeavor that makes it difficult for students to eat and ultimately loses money for the university.

Our team investigated the issue of inefficient checkout lines and designed a new system that included self-checkout machines. We developed a university-branded interface and tested it with 20 undergraduate students.

What was my role?

As a UX Researcher, I examined the current checkout process and observed everyday interactions between users and the system. Once we designed a replacement interface, I designed a protocol to obtain feedback from users and then conducted a thematic analysis of their experiences.

As a UI Designer, I crafted a new self-checkout interface for the university that modeled industry-standard implementations. I created high-fidelity UI prototypes that allowed users to test and interact with the proposed system.

Challenges

The campus's three primary dining options are the Davis Food Court, Coleman Commons in Oscar Peterson Hall, and Subway and Bento in the Instructional Center. Each location has three kiosks that require an employee to help students pay for their meals. Although the dining halls are configured for three kiosks, the locations are often manned by one or two people. In addition, while some dining halls have designated queues to order food, the lines to pay for food are unstructured and often obstruct access to other parts of the cafeteria. The combination of unmanned kiosks and obfuscated queues not only discourages students from eating but also disrupts a healthy eating schedule.

Objectives

  • Devise a system that improves the efficiency of dining hall checkout lines
  • Design a self-checkout user interface that is both familiar and easy to use

Solution

It is clear that there is plenty of room for improvement in the UTM food court distribution systems. Everything from long, numerous lines to a lack of services to enhance speed. The data collected this week echo our previous observations and clear patterns are beginning to emerge: the process of checking out at UTM dining halls are inefficient and have an impact on students’ lives. It all adds up to additional stress on students who may not have upwards of an hour to wait for lunch. Any solutions ideated towards fixing these issues will need to take into consideration the pain points identified in our analysis. Wait times, kiosks, and even payment methods can all be factors into ideating a more efficient system for both businesses and students alike in UTM food courts going forward. Ultimately the goal will be to increase speed, and reduce stress on students and workers during peak hours.

Through vigorous research and observations, we determined that the optimal solution was to implement self checkout stations throughout the campus’ dining facilities. The primary factor in long wait times was the availability of checkout stations. The first alternative considered was to simply add additional employees to serve customers, but as that is a systemic problem it is beyond the scope of our review. We opted for a self checkout because it did not rely on pure man-power, and even if there was only one person attending the checkouts it would be more than enough to serve any issues students face. Self checkouts allow for more than one station to be open at any given time, and as similar systems are a common occurrence in modern stores, there is little to no learning curve. The computer interface of our self checkout system mirrors that of systems found in other stores, both following the industry standard and allowing synergy between the two systems: when a customer learns one interface, they can comfortably operate another.

In order for the self checkout system to be fully effective, we recommend that UTM add at least two self checkouts to every location selling food – and, in high traffic areas such as Coleman Commons, we suggest three to four self checkout stations. Our observations highlighted the fact that a single checkout station is insufficient for the number of students purchasing food, and during peak hours in busy locations a line may still form with two open stations.
