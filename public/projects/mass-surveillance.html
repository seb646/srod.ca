Introduction

Overview

Mass surveillance is a pervasive and systemic threat to the rights and privacy of individuals across the world. In the United States, a complex system of laws and legal precedents established and continues to support the surveillance state. The government has warrantless access to millions of citizens' data, and they operate with little to no oversight. The practices and impacts of mass surveillance in the United States has been extensively documented, but it remains a challenging concept to fully comprehend.

The United States of Surveillance is a combination of an interactive website and academic paper that provides a brief overview and analysis of the most invasive practices and the complex legal structure that supports it. The website was presented as a digital poster at the Digital Humanities Summer Institute's 2021 Conference and Colloquium, and the paper has been submitted for review to an academic, peer-reviewed journal.

Paper Abstract

After the September 11 attacks, the United States government implemented several programs to safeguard its citizens both at home and abroad. The Global War on Terrorism began, and the power of the United States executive branch grew, deploying troops and declaring war on an enemy with no one country to call home. The fight against terrorism, however, was not limited to military deployments in the Middle East. Legislation and legal interpretations, including the USA PATRIOT Act, the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act (FISA), and others, granted the executive extraordinary powers to fight a digital surveillance war, not against any one country but against various networks of alleged terrorist cells located around the world.

The attacks on U.S. soil highlighted a lack of communication between the country's intelligence agencies. Programs implemented after 2001 sought to allow information to flow across various agencies, while greatly expanding the powers of intelligence gathering. Utilizing the Unitary Executive interpretation of Constitutional power, and with the help of legislation and a secret court, the executive branch of the United States government created a surveillance state that continues to jeopardize the rights American citizens have to privacy, the Fourth Amendment's protection from unreasonable searches and seizures, and the Sixth Amendment's rights to be informed when accused of a crime and to face an accuser in court. The pervasive and interlocking network of mass surveillance developing from the War on Terror has grown into a systemic problem for U.S. citizens, with its programs continuing to infringe upon the rights of millions while producing little to no meaningful results.

Digital Resource

The digital component of this project is an open-source website built with Vue.js that documents the laws, programs, and legal precedents that support mass surveillance in the United States. The website emulates a developer documentation website, with digestible pages outlining each separate section of the research paper.

As an open-source project, anyone is welcome to contribute to the repository through the project's GitHub. The rules for contributions, and detailed steps on how to contribute, are outlined on the website.
