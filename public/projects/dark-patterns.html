Challenge

Our actions on the internet are guided by the user interfaces and experiences crafted by designers and developers. A growing trend in web design are dark patterns (also known as "deceptive design" or "persuasive design"), which are a set of strategies that purposefully maipulate users into making decisions that go against their interests.

The responsibility of researchers and educators is to inform the next generation of designers, developers, and marketing strategists on the negative impact of dark patterns–including their inaccessiblility–and provide them with the tools required to create ethical experiences.

Solution

Develop an interactive, pedagogical website that teaches students to think critically about their internet activity by training them to identify common dark patterns. By educating the next generation of designers, developers, and business professionals, we aim to prevent the continued use of dark patterns in future design and marketing strategies.

UI Highlights

The design of the user interface was intended to be modern and simple, emulating experiences seen on other websites. The website features a section allowing users to read more about dark patterns and a fully emmersive e-commerce environment that tests student comprehension.

Impact

  • Integration into the curriculum of "Contemporary Communication Technologies" (CCT109), a first-year undergraduate course at the University of Toronto that educates ~800 students every year and is required for students studying design, web development, marketing, and certain business management programs.
  • Presented at the University of Toronto Critical Digital Humanities Initiative's Emerging Scholar Schowcase, a forum for post-doctoral, graduate, and undergraduate research fellows to share their work.
  • Received inquiries by other professors in higher education to incorporate the website into their course curriculums.
