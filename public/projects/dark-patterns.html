Introduction

What are dark patterns?

Dark patterns (also known as “deceptive design” or "persuasive design”) are a set of strategies which digital marketing and human-computer interaction (HCI) experts deploy in website development and design. Dark patterns are purposefully crafted design choices which lead users to make unintentional (and often malicious) decisions that go against their own interests.

What did we do?

We created a website based on months of research that demonstrates the dark patterns commonly found on social media and e-commerce websites. The purpose of the project was to develop an interactive, pedagogical tool that teaches first-year undergraduate students about manipulation and marketing strategies found in UI/UX design.

What was my role?

My primary responsibility was serving as lead developer and designer of the website. I custom-built each pattern (forms, pop-ups, etc) and integrated the designs with a WordPress website. I created a e-commerce micro-site using WooCommerce and created a simulated environment for students to test their knowledge of dark patterns. As a Research Fellow, I also discovered current scholarly work related to dark patterns and assisted in the development of CCT109's curriculum.

Objectives

Our goal was to create a website that effectively communicates and demonstrates dark patterns in user experience and interface design. Our target audience was first-year undergraduate students in the University of Toronto's communication, culture and technology stream, who are preparing for futures as designers, developers, business consultants, and marketing strategists.

Throughout our research, we defined the following core principles that our project required:

  • Educational: It provide students with a foundational understanding of UI/UX design, marketing strategies, and the psychology of manipulation.
  • Interactive: It must provide students with an experience that mirrors common blogs, shops, and social media websites.
  • Accessible: It must be usable by any student, regardless of their ability or screen size.
  • Relevant: It should highlight patterns that students likely experience on a regular basis.
  • Historical: It must provide students with a proper context, highlighting the evolution of design and marketing strategies.
  • Neutral: It must discuss dark patterns as a general field of study, and not explicitly name any one company, organization, or person.

Solution

Designing an educational website for first-year students required an intricate combination of cross-platform compatibility, accessible functions, and concise yet educational language. The website was designed to showcase individual dark patterns, providing a brief history on each strategy and allowing students to interact with examples. We also created a simulated e-commerce environment combining several dark patterns into a single platform, providing a space for students to test their knowledge and helping them identify unethical designs.

Outcome

While still in active development, this project will be integrated into the curriculum of CCT109, a first-year undergraduate course at the University of Toronto that is required for all students in the communication, culture and technology stream. CCT109 educates roughly 800 students every year and is designed to provide an introduction to society's current technological landscape. The project effectively combines design, marketing, and business strategies into a digestible introduction to the Institute of Communication, Culture, Information & Technology.

Dark patterns remain a present and dangerous threat to users on the internet, and they have the capability of manipulating users without their knowledge. By educating the next generation of designers, developers, and business professionals, we aim to prevent the continued use of dark patterns in future design and marketing strategies.
